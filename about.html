What is Racial Literacy?

Racial literacy is the ability to read, recast, and resolve racially stressful or identity-based encounters. This literacy is developed through supportive and repeated practice of healthy racial coping behaviors. The training of racial literacy involves the creation of humane spaces that provide affirming and protective feedback that also holds participants accountable as they learn to build their racial confidence, reduce their stress during racially charged moments, and negotiate racial conflicts to healthy conclusions.

Our Approach

While we are aware of and challenge systemic social injustices that shape our communities, we take a more proximal approach to racial conflict resolution. We teach skills that address face-to-face and in-the-moment racial encounters. We expect this work to help participants improve the quality of their racial interactions with colleagues, family members, and friendships; mature their racial identity development, and find their (social justice) voice by strengthening the link between their ideas and actions. We work with all age groups from youth to adults, across various sectors of education, health, justice and business, and across populations of front-line, middle management, and administrative leaders and professionals. By leveraging the power of storytelling and racial literacy skill development, we expect to promote emotional and relationship health on multiple levels. Our direct work begins with practitioners, staff and leaders of organizations, which indirectly impacts their client base, students and/or community. Eventually, we will deliver the direct face-to-face training to the clients (ex. students) themselves. We expect Lion’s Story will cultivate healthy climates and teach practices that influence activists, professionals, families and youth to recognize and resist narratives of racial dehumanization about their histories, cultures, and genius reconnect to and assert their own narratives of trauma and healing recover from the trauma of racism and improve their emotional, psychological, and physical coping and health and record and share the impact of applying these racial coping health processes and behaviors to everyday encounters.

Team

Dr. Howard C. Stevenson

Founder

Dr. Howard Stevenson is a nationally recognized clinical psychologist and researcher on negotiating racial conflicts using racial literacy for schools, community mental health centers, teachers, leaders, police and parents. He serves as the Executive Director of the Racial Empowerment Collaborative (REC) at Penn, which is designed to promote racial literacy research, training, and programming in education, health, community and justice institutions. Dr. Stevenson is also the Constance Clayton Professor of Urban Education, Professor of Africana Studies, in the Human Development & Quantitative Methods Division of the Graduate School of Education at the University of Pennsylvania.

Howard also directs Forward Promise, a national philanthropic office that promotes a culture of health for boys and young men of color, to help them heal from the trauma of historical and present-day dehumanization, discrimination and colonization. His book, Promoting Racial Literacy in Schools: Differences that Make a Difference, is designed to reduce racial threat reactions in face-to-face encounters. Howard’s research and clinical work have been funded by W.T. Grant Foundation, Annenberg Foundation, and the National Institutes of Mental Health and Child Health and Human Development.

Dr. Brian Peterson

Founder

Dr. Brian Peterson is the Director of the Makuu Black Cultural Center and a lecturer at the University of Pennsylvania. Dr. Peterson’s research interests include urban youth and communities, college student development, learning systems, and nonprofit organization development. In 1999, Dr. Peterson co-founded Asé Academy, an academic and cultural enrichment program for secondary students in Philadelphia. Four years later, Dr. Peterson created Lion’s Story with Dr. Stevenson to serve a nonprofit educational research and development group to support Asé’s growth and nurture other projects connected to education and culture.

Dr. Peterson is the author of four books: Move Over, Girl (2000), Spoken Words (2003), The African American Student’s Guide to Excellence in College (2005), and Higher Learning: Maximizing Your College Experience (2010).

Charles B. Adams

Managing Partner

For over twenty years, Charles has worked on behalf of children in schools and not-for-profit settings. As a former district school leader, Charles has worked in New York and Washington, DC, leading innovative school environments that upgraded the expectations for both schools and students alike. Most recently, Charles served as the Executive Director for the Greater Philadelphia Region of Teach For America. As a Founder and Managing Partner for Lion’s Story, Charles hopes to leverage the power of education and elevate racial literacy as an effective tool to fuel and fight injustice.

Charles has a Bachelors of Science from the Wharton School at the University of Pennsylvania, a Juris Doctorate from The Howard University School of Law, and a Masters of Education from Baruch College - The City University of New York. He also is licensed to practice law in Pennsylvania and serve as a school administrator.

Shamm Petros

Partner and Director of Learning & Evaluation

As a refugee from Eritrea, Shamm Petros is committed to supporting the psychological well-being and development of migrant children across the world. Shamm has several years of experience conducting extensive research for program development, leading project design, securing funding, and engineering tools for various refugee protection and education programs across the US, Kenya, Ethiopia, Uganda and Iraq. Additionally, she has provided direct clinical services to African-Americans and forced migrants affected by complex and sexual trauma. Her unique experience in international refugee relief services has prepared her to provide direct clinical services, as well as lead the design of trauma-informed, culturally relevant psychosocial interventions for various populations.

Shamm has also served as the Clinical Research Coordinator of the Racial Empowerment Collaborative (REC) at the University of Pennsylvania, leading the delivery, monitoring and evaluation of a school-based cognitive-behavioral trauma intervention. Ms. Petros holds an M.S.Ed in Counseling and Mental Health Services, M.Phil. in Professional Counseling.

Morgan Reid

Morgan gained her B.S. in Psychology from Villanova University and her M.S.Ed in Counseling and Mental Health Services from the University of Pennsylvania Graduate School of Education. Trainers

Dr. Kelsey Jones

Dr. Kelsey M. Jones is an Assistant Professor of Human Development at California State University, San Marcos. She received her Ph.D. in Interdisciplinary Studies in Human Development from the University of Pennsylvania Graduate School of Education, where she also completed a postdoctoral fellowship in the Department of Human Development and Quantitative Methods.

Dr. Jones engages in qualitative research that focuses on dis/ability and giftedness in the narratives of and about Black and Brown youth. Specifically, she studies the relationship between race, dis/ability, and the school-to-prison pipeline; and racial literacy education as healing for children and adults in stressful teaching and learning environments.

Dr. Marsha Richardson

Dr. Marsha Richardson is a clinical psychologist and a Senior Lecturer at the University of Pennsylvania’s Graduate School of Education’s Community and Mental Health Services program. She has over 30 years of experience providing clinical services, administrative leadership and professional development trainings in a variety of educational and clinical programs for children and adults in the Philadelphia area. She provides consultative services to schools, universities, and community-based programs in the areas of professional self-care, cultural diversity, and mental health-related issues. Her focus is on facilitating awareness and skills development for educators, service providers, staff, community leaders, and parents in emotionally resolving face-to-face racially and other marginalized identities that fuel social conflicts and undermine emotional well-being.
