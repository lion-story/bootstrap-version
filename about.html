What is Racial Literacy?

Racial literacy is the ability to read, recast, and resolve racially stressful or identity-based encounters. This literacy is developed through supportive and repeated practice of healthy racial coping behaviors. The training of racial literacy involves the creation of humane spaces that provide affirming and protective feedback that also holds participants accountable as they learn to build their racial confidence, reduce their stress during racially charged moments, and negotiate racial conflicts to healthy conclusions.

Our Approach

While we are aware of and challenge systemic social injustices that shape our communities, we take a more proximal approach to racial conflict resolution. We teach skills that address face-to-face and in-the-moment racial encounters. We expect this work to help participants improve the quality of their racial interactions with colleagues, family members, and friendships; mature their racial identity development, and find their (social justice) voice by strengthening the link between their ideas and actions. We work with all age groups from youth to adults, across various sectors of education, health, justice and business, and across populations of front-line, middle management, and administrative leaders and professionals. By leveraging the power of storytelling and racial literacy skill development, we expect to promote emotional and relationship health on multiple levels. Our direct work begins with practitioners, staff and leaders of organizations, which indirectly impacts their client base, students and/or community. Eventually, we will deliver the direct face-to-face training to the clients (ex. students) themselves. We expect Lion’s Story will cultivate healthy climates and teach practices that influence activists, professionals, families and youth to recognize and resist narratives of racial dehumanization about their histories, cultures, and genius reconnect to and assert their own narratives of trauma and healing recover from the trauma of racism and improve their emotional, psychological, and physical coping and health and record and share the impact of applying these racial coping health processes and behaviors to everyday encounters.

Team

Dr Howard C. Stevenson

Founder

Dr. Howard Stevenson is the Constance Clayton Professor of Urban Education, Professor of Africana Studies, in the Human Development & Quantitative Methods Division of the Graduate School of Education at the University of Pennsylvania. He is the Executive Director of the Racial Empowerment Collaborative at Penn, designed to promote racial literacy research, training, and programming in education, health, community and justice institutions.
Backed by a $12 million grant from the Robert Wood Johnson Foundation and based at REC, Howard also directs Forward Promise, a national philanthropic office that promotes a culture of health for boys and young men of color, to help them heal from the trauma of historical and present-day dehumanization, discrimination and colonization.
He is a nationally recognized clinical psychologist and researcher on negotiating racial conflicts using racial literacy for independent and public K-12 schooling, community mental health centers, teachers, leaders, police and parents. Two mental health research projects funded by National Institutes of Health examine the benefits of racial literacy. The PLAAY (Preventing Long-term Anger and Aggression in Youth) Project uses the physical activity of basketball and the literacy skills building of racial socialization to help youth and parents cope with stress from violence and social dehumanization. Dr. Stevenson also co-leads with Drs. Loretta and John Jemmott, the SHAPE-UP: Barbers Building Better Brothers Project which trains Black barbers as health educators to teach Black 18-24 year old males to reduce their risk of -- HIV/STDS and retaliation violence -- while they are cutting hair.
His book, Promoting Racial Literacy in Schools: Differences that Make a Difference, is designed to reduce racial threat reactions in face-to-face encounters. Howard’s research and clinical work have been funded by W.T. Grant Foundation, Annenberg Foundation, and the National Institutes of Mental Health and Child Health and Human Development.

Charles B. Adams

Managing Partner

For over twenty years, Charles worked on behalf of children in schools and not-for- profit settings. His personal educational background traces a K-12 arc as a student of several urban public schools and an A Better Chance student at an independent school. His higher education path wound through the Ivy League, a historically Black university’s law school, and a state school. Starting as an AmeriCorps member in the traditionally African-American Shaw neighborhood in Washington, DC, Charles returned to New York and unintentionally became an educator himself Without the formulaic five-year plan, yet well aware of the educational hurdles and roadblocks facing children and communities, Charles began working in a Brooklyn middle school just a short subway ride from his mother’s childhood home.
In hindsight, Charles now sees himself as an unintentional storm chaser of urban education reform. As a district school leader, he worked in New York schools under Mayor Bloomberg and Chancellor Joel Klein. During Michelle Rhee’s Washington, DC tenure, he led an innovative, college preparatory, public charter boarding school as the entire city upgraded the expectations for both schools and students. So now, after heading Teach For America’s Greater Philadelphia Region supporting the talent efforts of public schools, he is in Philadelphia with Dr. Hite, mayoral control and the City’s newly formed, local Board of Education.
According to a framework created by Gloria Ladson-Billings, Charles considers himself a coach and a conductor who sees education as one effective tool to fuel and fight injustice. Charles now teaches an undergraduate education course focused on Education in American Culture and also a graduate class on cultural sustaining pedagogy. Charles also consults with the School of District of Philadelphia and area not for profits.
Charles has a Bachelors of Science from the Wharton School at the University of Pennsylvania, a Juris Doctorate from The Howard University School of Law and a Masters of Education from Baruch College - The City University of New York. He also is licensed to practice law in Pennsylvania and serve as a school administrator.

Shamm Petros

Partner - Learning & Evaluation

As a refugee from Eritrea, Shamm Petros, is committed to supporting the psychological well-being and development of migrant children across the world. She has six years of experience conducting extensive research for program development, leading project design, securing eight million USD in funding and engineering tools for various refugee protection and education programs across the USA, Kenya, Ethiopia, Uganda and Iraq.
Additionally, Ms. Petros has provided direct clinical services to African-Americans and forced migrants affected by complex and sexual trauma. Her unique experience in international refugee relief services has prepared her to provide direct clinical services and lead the design and execution of trauma-informed, culturally relevant psychosocial interventions for various populations of concern.
Ms. Petros hopes to study the effects of trauma on children and families, in order to prepare institutions such as schools, hospitals, community based organizations and government agencies to provide adequate counseling and education support for vulnerable youth such as refugees, forced migrants, children involved in the juvenile system, survivors of trauma, and other vulnerable populations. Currently, Shamm is the Clinical Research Coordinator of the Racial Empowerment Collaborative at the University of Pennsylvania, leading the delivery, monitoring and evaluation of a school-based cognitive-behavioral trauma intervention. Additionally, she serves as the Partner and Director of Learning and Evaluation for Lion’s Story.
Ms. Petros holds an M.S.Ed in Counseling and Mental Health Services, M.Phil. in Professional Counseling.

Trainers

Dr. Kelsey Jones

Dr. Kelsey M. Jones is an Assistant Professor of Human Development at California State University, San Marcos. She received her Ph.D. in Interdisciplinary Studies in Human Development from the University of Pennsylvania Graduate School of Education, where she also completed a postdoctoral fellowship in the department of Human Development and Quantitative Methods.
Dr. Jones engages in qualitative research that focuses on dis/ability and giftedness in the narratives of and about Black and Brown youth. Specifically, she studies the relationship between race, dis/ability, and the school-to-prison pipeline; and racial literacy education as healing for children and adults in stressful teaching and learning environments. Her current research program, Preparing Educators to Address Racial Literacy and Stress (PEARLS), includes 1) a professional development-to- participatory action research series designed to support educators who want to build more racially literate practices and 2) the development of accessible racial literacy materials and curriculum for children, adolescents, families, and schools.

Dr. Marsha Richardson

Marsha Richardson, PsyD is a clinical psychologist by training and a Senior Lecturer at the University of Pennsylvania’s Graduate School of Education’s Community and Mental Health Services program. She has over 30 years of experience providing clinical services, administrative leadership and professional development trainings in a variety of educational and clinical programs for children and adults in the Philadelphia area. She provides consultative services to schools, universities, and community-based programs in the areas of professional self-care, cultural diversity, and mental health-related issues. She is a team member and trainer with Dr. Howard Stevenson’s Racial Empowerment Collaborative (REC) and the Lion’s Story organizations whose focus is on facilitating awareness and skills development for educators, service providers, staff, community leaders, and parents in emotionally resolving face-to-face racially and other marginalized identities that fuel social conflicts and undermine emotional well-being.
Dr. Richardson facilitates courageous conversations between educators, students, and parents on a variety of tough topics in school settings across the region through SpeakUp! and she has a small private practice. In addition to these external endeavors, Dr. Richardson consults with several Penn programs and within the Graduate School of Education on such topics as trauma sensitive pedagogy and student self-care/wellness. She is also a certified Youth and Mental Health First Aid trainer who provides mental health awareness trainings in local schools and non-profit organizations.
